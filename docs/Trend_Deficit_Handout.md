# Trend-Implied Deficit: A Quick Field Guide

## The Point

Body weight changes are noisy day to day. Water, sodium, glycogen, food volume, soreness, and sleep can move the scale even when fat loss is still happening.

The better question is not:

> "What did I weigh today?"

The better question is:

> "What is my average weight doing over time?"

This handout explains a simple way to estimate whether a nutrition plan is working by comparing 7-day average body weight, waist measurements, and training performance.

---

## The Simple Math

A common rough estimate is:

```text
1 lb body weight change ~= 3,500 kcal
```

So:

```text
weekly weight loss in lb * 3,500 = estimated weekly deficit
estimated weekly deficit / 7 = estimated daily deficit
```

Example:

```text
2.2 lb lost per week * 3,500 = 7,700 kcal/week
7,700 / 7 = 1,100 kcal/day
```

That does not mean the number is perfect. It means the weight trend currently behaves like about a 1,100 kcal/day deficit.

---

## Why Use 7-Day Averages?

Daily scale weight can lie.

Example daily weights:

```text
Day:      Mon   Tue   Wed   Thu   Fri   Sat   Sun
Weight:  198   196   197   195   196   194   195
```

The line jumps around, but the average tells the truth better:

```text
7-day average = 195.9 lb
```

Now compare that to the next 7-day average:

```text
Prior 7-day average:   195.9 lb
Latest 7-day average:  193.7 lb
Change:                -2.2 lb/week
```

Estimated deficit:

```text
2.2 * 3,500 / 7 = 1,100 kcal/day
```

---

## What Waist Adds

Waist does not calculate calories directly, but it helps confirm whether the weight trend is meaningful.

Use waist as a sanity check:

```text
Weight average down + waist average down = strong support
Weight average down + waist flat = still possible, keep watching
Weight average down + waist up = may be water, gut content, or measurement noise
Weight flat + waist down = recomposition or water masking fat loss
```

Quick example:

```text
Prior 7-day waist average:   36.4 in
Latest 7-day waist average:  35.9 in
Change:                     -0.5 in/week
```

That supports the idea that the weight trend is not just random scale noise.


The weekly view is less exciting, but usually more honest.

## Example Interpretation

Suppose:

```text
Average intake:             1,400 kcal/day
Trend-implied deficit:      1,100 kcal/day
```

Estimated current daily energy expenditure:

```text
1,400 + 1,100 = 2,500 kcal/day
```

If an initial estimate was 2,800 kcal/day, the difference may come from:

- lower body weight
- less unconscious movement
- fatigue reducing activity
- less food digestion cost
- water/glycogen shifts
- normal error in the estimate

This is why the trend matters more than one weigh-in.

---

## What This Does Not Prove

This is not a lab measurement.

It does not perfectly separate:

- fat loss
- water loss
- glycogen loss
- gut content
- lean mass change
- day-to-day measurement error

The 3,500 kcal/lb rule is a useful rough estimate, but real bodies adapt. As calories drop and weight comes down, total daily energy expenditure often drops too.

---

## Practical Takeaway

If the plan is working, the pattern should look like this:

```text
7-day average weight: down
7-day average waist:  down or stable-to-down
training performance: maintained as well as possible
adherence: consistent
```

Do not panic over one day.

Watch the trend.
